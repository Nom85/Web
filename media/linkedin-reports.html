The "Finprint" — How Photo-ID Transforms Cetacean Research

Photo-ID Citizen Science Happywhale Marine Conservation

During work with the MARE-Madeira team, I learned how researchers identify each cetacean through Photo-ID — a powerful technique that uses the unique marks, scars and shape of dorsal fins or tail flukes to recognize individuals over time.

Key Insights

  • Every whale has a unique "finprint" — just like fingerprints
  • A clear image is a valuable data point, not just a beautiful picture
  • Platforms like Happywhale use AI to compare photos with global databases
  • Scientists can track migrations, behaviors and population health

This experience showed me how a single photo can transform our understanding of the ocean. Citizen science platforms are democratizing cetacean research, allowing anyone with a camera to contribute to marine science.

GPS Tracking & Bio-Residual Sampling — Tools Behind Cetacean Research

GPS Tracking Bio-Residual Sampling Marine Megafauna Research Methods

While working with the MARE-Madeira team, I had the opportunity to learn about two powerful research techniques: GPS/satellite tracking and the collection of bio-residual samples (hair, skin, trace fragments).

GPS/Satellite Tracking

  • Maps migration corridors and habitat use
  • Detects overlaps with shipping lanes and fishing activity
  • Improves conservation measure design

Bio-Residual Sampling

  • Measures contaminant levels in marine organisms
  • Reveals diet and trophic position through analysis
  • Evaluates overall health via small biopsy samples

Combining tracking data with biological samples allows researchers to move from observing patterns to understanding processes — asking not just where a whale goes, but why.

Identifying Whales by Their Blow — What the Spout Reveals

Whale Watching Field Identification Marine Biology Sperm Whale

Out at sea, sometimes you don't see the fin or the tail. Only a burst of mist rising from the surface. That moment — the blow — reveals more than you think. Marine observers use these subtle differences to identify species even from miles away.

Types of Blows

  • Rorqual whales (blue, fin, humpback): tall, straight column — can reach 9+ meters
  • Right whales: distinctive V-shaped spout from two widely-separated blowholes
  • Sperm whales: angled blow, ~45° to the left — because their single blowhole sits asymmetrically on the left side of the head

Next time you see a puff on the horizon, remember: that's not just air — it's a clue to who's breathing beneath the waves.

The Loggerhead Turtle — A Journey Home Guided by Earth's Magnetic Field

Caretta caretta Marine Conservation Sea Turtles Biodiversity

Caretta caretta can travel more than 12,000 km from the shores where it hatches to the very same spot where it returns decades later to lay its eggs — guided only by the Earth's magnetic field. No compass, no GPS.

Loggerhead Turtle Facts

  • Can dive over 200 meters and stay underwater for up to 4 hours
  • Only 1 in every 1,000 hatchlings reaches adulthood
  • Play a key role in maintaining the health of sandy seabeds
  • Common threats: boat strikes and floating debris

Watching a Caretta caretta is a lesson in patience and resilience. In a world dominated by speed and noise, creatures like sea turtles remind us that success lies in consistency.

The Most Elusive Cetacean — Cuvier's Beaked Whale

Ziphius cavirostris Beaked Whale Deep Diving World Record

The most elusive cetacean in the ocean: Ziphius cavirostris (Cuvier's beaked whale). Three silent individuals appeared during fieldwork in Madeira — a species that holds two remarkable world records.

World Records

  • Deepest dive ever recorded for a mammal: 2,992 m
  • Longest time underwater: up to 3h 42min

Why So Elusive?

  • Live in small groups
  • Extremely elusive behavior
  • Spend most of their lives in deep offshore waters
  • Scars and marks from competition, cephalopod interactions, and parasites accumulate with age

The Dolphin Almost No One Knows — Steno bredanensis

Steno bredanensis Rough-toothed Dolphin Biodiversity Deep Water Species

You've probably never seen it. Yet Steno bredanensis roams tropical and subtropical seas all around the planet. It lives far from the coast, in deep waters (1,500–2,000 meters), forming groups of 10–30 individuals.

What Makes It Unique

  • Only living member of the Steno genus — an evolutionary rarity
  • Rough-surfaced teeth (20–27 per jaw) with visible ridges — designed to grip prey at depth
  • Head profile unlike any other dolphin — no typical forehead, blends into a long narrow beak
  • Dark grey back, pinkish-white belly; coloration becomes yellowish with age

This is why it remains so mysterious — its preference for deep waters makes it incredibly difficult to study.

Why Do Cetaceans Really Jump?

Wildlife Behavior Cetacean Ecology Science Communication Marine Biology

It's not just a method of movement — each type of jump serves a different function. Cetaceans don't jump to entertain us. They jump to communicate, compete, survive, and learn.

Reasons Cetaceans Jump

  • Energy efficiency: Small cetaceans alternate swimming and jumping; bow-riding can reduce respiratory rate by up to 45%
  • Long-distance communication: A humpback breach creates an acoustic signal traveling kilometers underwater
  • Parasite removal: The impact against the water surface dislodges ectoparasites
  • Territory and display: During breeding, males breach to demonstrate physical condition
  • Play and learning: Calves practice motor skills; mothers and young strengthen bonds through play
